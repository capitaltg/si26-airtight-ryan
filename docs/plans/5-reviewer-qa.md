# Airtight: reviewer Q&A (the hard questions, answered)

_Airtight is software where a company bidding on a US government contract can rehearse the live question-and-answer part of an "oral proposal" against AI evaluator characters, and get a score they can defend afterward. This doc preps you to talk that pitch through with a skeptical reviewer. Each entry has three parts: the question the way a sharp reviewer actually asks it, a crisp answer, and a rebuttal for the follow-up they'll throw next. Where the honest answer is a concession plus a plan, it says so. A good reviewer trusts "here's the risk and how we retire it" more than bluster._

_Detail in [1-overview.md](1-overview.md), [2-scoring-and-drift.md](2-scoring-and-drift.md), [3-spec.md](3-spec.md), [4-chatbot-comparison.md](4-chatbot-comparison.md)._

## The scoring moat

### Q1. "Does the buyer actually want a defensible number, or do they just want good feedback?"

**Answer.** Both, and the number isn't the deliverable. The after-action report is. What lands on the buyer's desk is a diagnostic: how many questions were dodged, what was answered fully versus partially, where an answer contradicted the written proposal, and an explanation of the grade, generated by code and tied to exact quotes. That's the same thing a gold team hands back (a gold team is the mock panel of senior colleagues who role-play the government evaluators), except this version is reproducible and available on demand. Determinism isn't the sales pitch by itself. It's what lets the diagnostic be trusted and re-run instead of re-argued.

**Rebuttal to "so it's a feedback tool, drop the determinism talk."** The determinism is why the feedback survives contact with a proposal shop. These buyers live on evaluation criteria, and the first thing they do with a grade is challenge it. A report you can replay line-by-line against the scoring rules ends that argument. A chatbot's feedback restarts it every turn.

### Q2. "Determinism isn't validity. Has anyone shown the score predicts a real outcome?"

**Answer.** We don't claim it predicts win probability. We explicitly disclaim that (doc 2: "not a probability of real-world success"). The claim is narrower and defensible: the score is a faithful, reproducible tally of behaviors a domain expert decided matter, traced back to the presenter's own words. Validity comes in three layers. First, face validity: CTG's orals experts author and sign off on the rubric (the fixed scoring rules) and the red lines. Second, controlled behavior: a 15 to 20 hand-graded exchange golden set regression-tests that the same answer scores consistently. Third, outcome correlation: after the proof-of-concept, we score sessions against real gold-team ratings and, eventually, against actual contract awards.

**Rebuttal to "then it's just a fancy opinion."** It's a stable, inspectable expert opinion, which is exactly what a gold team is, except a gold team's opinion shifts with who's in the room and can't be re-run. We're not claiming an objectivity the rubric doesn't have. We're claiming the rubric is explicit, disclosed, and applied the same way every time. That's more than the human process offers, not less.

### Q3. "Haven't you just moved the model's judgment one layer down, not removed it?"

**Answer.** Yes, deliberately, and that's the design rather than a dodge. We moved the model off the job of "invent a 0 to 100 number" (which it does badly and inconsistently) and onto "classify this answer into a few labels, each backed by a required quote" (which it does well and stably). Judgment isn't eliminated. It's shrunk to its most reliable form, and everything downstream of it is deterministic arithmetic plus an auditable trace.

**Rebuttal to "so the reliability problem is still there, just hidden."** It's not hidden, it's measured. The whole interpretive surface is now "did this answer land in `full/partial/none` and `bare/specified/backed`." That's a small, testable space, and the golden-set regression test targets exactly it. Compare that to a chatbot re-deriving a number over a full transcript, where the variation surface is unbounded and untestable.

### Q4. "Temperature 0 doesn't make an LLM deterministic. Your 'same answer, same score' claim is shaky."

**Answer.** Correct, and we don't rest on temperature 0. (Temperature is the model's randomness setting; 0 is the least random.) It reduces sampling variance but doesn't guarantee bit-identical output across batches or provider changes. The claim rests on three other things. First, output is constrained to a fixed set of allowed values in a forced schema, so there's almost no free text for variance to live in. Second, the arithmetic layer is actually deterministic: same extraction in, same number out, guaranteed. Third, the golden-set regression test is a shipped deliverable, not an assumption: it reruns on every change, so a drift in classification shows up before it ships. If extraction drifts on a case, we add a worked example until it settles. The honest framing is controlled, regression-tested behavior, not a promise of bit-identical output.

**Rebuttal to "so it's 'approximately deterministic,' same as the chatbot column."** No. The difference is where the leftover variance can reach. In a chatbot the variance reaches the number itself. In Airtight it's fenced into a narrow classification step, and the number is a pure function of that step. "The label occasionally flips on a hard answer" is a bug we can find and fix with an example. "The grade is re-improvised every turn" is architecture, and you can't fix architecture with a prompt.

### The report authorship fork (follow-on to Q1)

**"Who writes the report, code or the model?"** Both, with a bright line between them, and that line is the point. The report has two parts:

- **The scored part is rendered by code.** The counts (dodges, coverage full/partial/none), the grade, the grade explanation, and the **grading/classification summary**, for example *"8 concerns raised: 3 answered fully, 2 partial, 3 dodged; 1 contradiction with the written proposal (staffing); meter 62, no red line crossed"*, are all assembled by code from the extraction, the `matched_rows` (the rubric rows that fired), and the quoted spans. Deterministic and replayable.
- **The narrative recap is written by the model and explicitly labeled "not scored."** A short readable pass, for example *"you opened strong on architecture but wobbled when Marcus pressed on cost realism"*, is generated the same way the in-character reply is. It's commentary, marked as such in the report, and it never feeds the number.

**Rebuttal to "so a model still writes part of the report, isn't that the chatbot storytelling you told me not to trust?"** No, and the label is why. The recap sits under a "not scored" header precisely so it can be readable without touching the grade. Everything above it, every count, the grade, the explanation, is rendered by code from quoted evidence and replays identically. We didn't ban model prose. We fenced it off from the number and said so on the page. The rule holds: the model classifies and speaks in character; code counts, scores, and explains. The one requirement is that the label is visible in the report and UI, not just asserted here.

## Software or a consulting service?

### Q5. "The content is all hand-authored per pursuit. Isn't the real product CTG labor, not software?"

**Answer.** Near-term, yes, and for a first product that's a feature rather than a bug. The engine (pipeline, scoring, anti-drift, audit) is software and doesn't change per client. The config (the personas, the concerns, rubric calibration, reference docs) is expert labor today. We ship the first revenue as a CTG-delivered service precisely because it earns real usage before we bet on automating ingestion. The roadmap retires the labor as RFP ingestion matures (the RFP is the government's written solicitation, and ingestion means the software reading it automatically), and the margin improves as the config gets cheaper to produce.

**Rebuttal to "so it doesn't scale."** The engine scales, and the config is what we're driving toward self-serve. The config being expert-authored is a moat, not a liability: a generic AI shop can build the pipeline but can't author personas and a rubric that a govcon proposal manager (the person who owns a specific bid) finds credible. That domain content is the thing CTG has and they don't.

### Q6. "You only rehearse the questions you thought of. The real risk at orals is the curveball."

**Answer.** True for the proof-of-concept's frozen concern bank, and that's the right scope to prove the core. Two things soften it even in the POC. The sub-question structure lets a persona push follow-ups within a concern, so it isn't a fixed script. And the hardest questions in orals are usually anticipated but fumbled rather than truly novel: teams know "how do you take over from the incumbent without disruption" is coming and still wobble on it. Reps against those are valuable.

**Rebuttal to "but the surprise question is what sinks people."** Agreed, and generated, adaptive follow-ups are on the roadmap. We're not claiming the POC simulates surprise. We're claiming it drills the predictable-but-hard core, which is where most points are lost. Don't oversell the POC as covering the curveball.

## Market and business model

### Q7. "How big is 'orals-required federal bids,' actually?"

**Answer.** This is a real open question, and we should size it with CTG's pipeline data before the review rather than assert it. The sizing frame: (number of orals-required solicitations per year) times (addressable firms bidding each) times (B&P dollars per pursuit) times (realistic penetration). B&P is the bid-and-proposal budget a firm sets aside for a specific bid. Directionally, orals are common in large IT and services task orders across civilian and DoD agencies, and CTG sees this volume directly, which is why CTG is the right place to get a real number.

**Rebuttal to "so you don't know your market size."** Correct, and I'd rather tell you that than fabricate a total-addressable-market figure. The beachhead doesn't need a huge market to prove the POC. It needs one real pursuit from CTG's orbit. Sizing the full market is a fast follow using data CTG already has.

### Q8. "'Five figures is a rounding error against the award' isn't how buyers decide. And you can't prove the tool won the bid."

**Answer.** Two honest concessions and a reframe. We don't price at a percentage of the award. The award value is the ceiling that makes five figures palatable, not the basis. Actual price anchors against the alternative: a gold team burns loaded senior hours, which is real money the buyer already spends. And ROI per bid is unattributable, agreed, so we don't sell "we won you the bid." We sell two things that are attributable: unlimited reps at near-zero marginal cost, and senior gold-team hours handed back.

**Rebuttal to "B&P is scrutinized, they won't spend it on an unproven tool."** That scrutiny is exactly why pursuit-prep framing beats training-budget framing. It's a line item on a live, funded bid, not a "someday" training ask. And the first sale is CTG-delivered against a specific pursuit, which is the lowest-friction way to spend B&P: a service tied to a named deal.

### Q9. "Once the bid closes, the value walks out the door, the same critique you make of gold teams. Why does anyone come back?"

**Answer.** Per-pursuit is the land, not the retention story. Retention lives with firms that bid orals-required work several times a year: mid-to-large primes with a proposal center. For them the pitch is consistency across the pipeline, and that's a subscription. For infrequent bidders we don't pretend there's recurring revenue. They're repeat per-pursuit buyers at best.

**Rebuttal to "so your market is just a handful of big primes."** The recurring-revenue market is. The per-pursuit market is broader. We're honest that the subscription expansion targets the segment with a real pipeline, and we size the two segments separately rather than blending them.

### Q10. "Running in the customer's own AWS account is a bespoke deployment per client. How is that ever self-serve SaaS?"

**Answer.** Two deployment models, matched to two segments, not one model stretched to cover both. Security-sensitive primes get the in-their-account deployment. That's the beachhead, and the security story is the whole reason we get in the door. Less-sensitive buyers later get a CTG-hosted multi-tenant version, still on AWS Bedrock (AWS's service for running AI models inside your own AWS account) with the same no-training and encryption guarantees. The tension is real, and we resolve it by segmenting rather than by pretending one model serves everyone.

**Rebuttal to "bespoke deployment kills your margin."** For the beachhead, deployment rides along with the config service we're already delivering. It isn't a separate cost center yet. Multi-tenant self-serve is what lowers deployment cost, and it's sequenced for the segment that doesn't require the in-account boundary.

## Claims to verify before the room

### Q11. "You claim GovCloud and FedRAMP High. Is that actually true today for the models you'd use?"

**Answer.** Verified July 2026, against AWS's own FedRAMP services-in-scope page and the GovCloud User Guide. (GovCloud is AWS's US government cloud region, and FedRAMP High and DoD IL4/5 are the security accreditations that let sensitive government workloads run there.) Amazon Bedrock is FedRAMP High authorized in AWS GovCloud (US), in both the US-West and US-East regions. Claude is authorized there under FedRAMP High and DoD IL4/5, and the GovCloud User Guide names Claude Sonnet 4.5 (among others) explicitly. So Airtight can run a current-generation frontier Claude model inside that authorized boundary. This is a confirmed fact, not a hope. One precision to keep: the FedRAMP High authorization covers the Bedrock platform and the model, not Airtight as an application. Airtight deploys into that boundary and still earns its own authorization to operate; we describe the target posture, not an accreditation the app inherits automatically. Two more disciplines around the claim. First, pin the model version we cite ("Claude Sonnet 4.5, confirmed July 2026"), because the authorized matrix changes. Second, the voice services (Nova Sonic, Transcribe, Polly) are a separate accreditation question, still to confirm, and they're post-POC anyway.

**Rebuttal to "so your central differentiator is unconfirmed."** It's confirmed for the text path the POC actually ships on. The architecture (inference in the customer's account, no training on prompts, private VPC, encryption) holds regardless, and the specific accreditation, Bedrock at FedRAMP High in GovCloud with Claude authorized, checks out against current AWS documentation. The one thing we don't yet assert is voice-service accreditation, and we say so rather than bluff it.

**A second security point we can make today (no verification pending).** Set the accreditation question aside entirely and there's still a hard line between us and consumer AI. Bedrock hosts frontier third-party models: Anthropic's Claude, Meta's Llama, Mistral, Cohere, alongside Amazon's own Nova and Titan. The value isn't that there's no third party. It's that **your prompts and proposal text never reach the model provider and are never used to train the model.** AWS mediates the call inside the customer's own account and region. So you get Claude-class quality without the consumer-app exposure that makes ChatGPT, Gemini, or Claude.ai a non-starter for a live proposal, where pasting win themes, pricing, or CUI (controlled unclassified information) into a vendor's boundary is the thing the buyer cannot do. This holds today, independent of the GovCloud and FedRAMP check above.

> **Say it this way, not that way.** ✅ "Your data never reaches the model provider and is never trained on." ❌ "Bedrock has no third-party providers" is false, and an AWS-literate reviewer will correct you on the spot. Bedrock's whole catalog is third-party models; the differentiator is data isolation, not their absence.

### Q12. "The number is path- and length-dependent, not a percentage, not comparable across sessions. What does the buyer do with it?"

**Answer.** They read the report, not the raw number. The number is ordinal within one session ("78 beats 64 beats 50 here"), and the meaning lives in the diagnostic around it. We already disclaim what the number isn't (doc 2). Normalization is a genuine open design question. The likely resolution is to report rate-based stats (dodges per N turns, coverage rate) that are length-independent, and to decide per-turn normalization before build.

**Rebuttal to "then don't lead with a score at all."** Fair pressure, and the report-first framing answers it. The headline is "you dodged 5 of 8 and contradicted your staffing plan once," not "you got a 71." The meter is the progress signal during practice; the report is the deliverable.

## Voice

### Q13. "You call voice the real product but build text. Aren't you proving the wrong thing in the POC?"

**Answer.** No, it's deliberate sequencing. The novel, unproven part is the defensible extraction-and-scoring pipeline (where the model reads an answer, labels it, and attaches a quote), and that part is identical whether the input is typed or transcribed. Voice is an input adapter in front of an unchanged core. Text-first proves the hard, never-been-done part at the lowest cost, without paying for real-time latency, audio cost, and transcription accuracy up front.

**Rebuttal to "but text lets the presenter edit away exactly what orals test."** True, and that's why voice is the top post-POC priority rather than a someday. Voice adds realism and engineering risk, not conceptual risk. We prove the concept in text, then add the realism once the scoring moat is demonstrated.

### Q14. "Voice's whole rationale is delivery, filler, freezing, evasive tone, but your rubric refuses to score delivery. So what does voice actually add?"

**Answer.** Good catch, and the honest answer tightens the voice pitch. Voice adds two things the rubric already values. It removes the backspace: you can't silently edit a ramble or a non-answer into a clean one. And it applies real-time pressure that surfaces the dodges and filler the conciseness signal already scores. What voice does not do today is grade tone, charisma, or delivery polish. The rubric explicitly stays out of that.

**Rebuttal to "then you're overselling voice."** We should tighten the language so it doesn't imply we grade delivery. The accurate claim: voice makes the rehearsal honest (no editing) and realistic (live pressure), and it feeds the same scorer. If we want to grade delivery signals like pauses, hedging, and pace, that's a deliberate rubric extension on the roadmap, and we'd say so rather than smuggle it into the voice claim.
